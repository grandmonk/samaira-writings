Bananas, Balloons, and the Price of Nothing

April 2024

There is a moment, just before a balloon slips from your hand, when it feels weightless but not yet gone. It hovers, suspended between possession and loss. Not held by force but by the illusion that you still control it. A child watches it drift upward, convinced for a second too long that reaching just a little higher will bring it back.

Bananas behave differently. They do not float or linger. They brown, soften, collapse inward on themselves. Left alone, they become something else entirely, no longer fruit, but a notion of the passing of time. No one mistakes a rotten banana for anything but what it is: value lost, visibly and undeniably.

We understand decay when we can see it.

We do not understand it nearly as well when it is hidden behind abstraction.

This is where things begin to blur.

In 2019, a banana taped to a wall was sold for $120,000. The fruit itself was not the point; it would be replaced as it rotted. The value was assigned to the idea, the certificate, the context. People argued over whether it was absurd or profound, but no one could argue that it was accidental. The price was deliberate. Someone decided what it was worth, and someone else agreed.

The balloon, too, is priced before it is lost. Not for its material, thin latex, a few cents at most, but for its experience. Its color, its lightness, the way it transforms air into something that feels like joy. We pay for the illusion that something so fragile can still feel full.

And then there are the things we cannot see at all.

Debt does not brown like a banana. It does not drift away like a balloon. It accumulates quietly, numbers layered onto numbers, abstract enough to feel distant and precise enough to feel permanent. It has no smell, no texture, no visible decay. And yet it shapes lives far more concretely than either fruit or air ever could.

A late fee is not experienced as a number. It is experienced as a narrowing: fewer choices, fewer exits, fewer ways to recover. Interest compounds not just financially, but psychologically. The longer something persists, the more inevitable it begins to feel.

What is striking is not that these systems exist, but how normal they feel.

We rarely question why a banana can be worthless in one context and priceless in another. We accept that a balloon is worth more before it slips away than after. But when it comes to financial systems like credit scores, interest rates, access to capital, we often treat them as if they are natural laws rather than constructed ones.

They are neither.

Like the banana on the wall, value is assigned.

Like the balloon in the air, it is sustained by belief.

The difference is that in financial systems, the consequences are not symbolic.

Consider how risk is measured. For some, it is an abstract calculation, a percentage, a likelihood, a line in a spreadsheet. For others, it is embodied: the risk of missing rent, of unexpected medical expenses, of a single misstep triggering a cascade of penalties. The system claims neutrality, but its effects are unevenly distributed. What appears objective from a distance feels deeply personal up close.

This is not a failure of mathematics. It is a reflection of priorities.

We have built systems that excel at assigning value but struggle to question it.

Why does access to capital depend so heavily on past access? Why are penalties structured in ways that amplify rather than absorb instability? Why do we treat financial “discipline” as an individual trait when the conditions that enable it are often structural?

These questions are not technical. They are philosophical.

They ask not how value is calculated, but why.

The absurdity is not that a banana can cost $120,000. The absurdity is that we find this more shocking than the everyday mechanisms that quietly extract wealth from those with the least of it. We recognize spectacle more easily than structure.

Absurdity, in this sense, becomes a kind of distraction. It draws attention to what is unusual while leaving the ordinary untouched. But the ordinary is where the real consequences live.

A balloon slipping from a child’s hand is a small loss. It is visible, immediate, and complete. The moment passes. The loss is understood.

Financial loss rarely works this way.

It is incremental and often reversible only in theory. It does not announce itself; it accumulates. And because it lacks the clarity of visible decay, it is easier to ignore, until it is not.

What would it mean to design systems that made value more visible, not less? To create structures where loss is not hidden in compounding terms and fine print, but recognized early, addressed collectively, and distributed more fairly?

Reform, at its core, is not about eliminating complexity. It is about deciding which complexities are necessary and which are obscuring something more important. We cannot eliminate abstraction from financial systems. But we can choose whether that abstraction serves clarity or conceals inequality.

The balloon will always drift upward.

The banana will always decay.

But the price we assign, what we choose to value, protect, or ignore, is not inevitable. It is constructed.

Meaning it can be changed.

Don't Blame the Player, Blame the Deck

April 2024

During any card game, there is always someone who loses the hand of cards. They looked at what they were holding. Not dramatically, just long enough to replay it. The “almosts” begin to stack: one card away, one decision earlier, one different turn. It doesn’t feel like losing. It feels like something slightly misaligned. And then, eventually, someone says: “Who shuffled!” Not accusing, not really.

What’s strange isn’t that people lose. It’s how quickly losing turns into a question about the system. No one asks about the shuffle when they’re winning. When the cards fall right, the game feels clean, almost easy. Outcomes make sense. Decisions feel like they matter.

It’s only when the pattern breaks against you that randomness starts to feel structured, like there’s an order you just weren’t let in on.

If you stay at the table long enough, you start to notice that people aren’t just playing cards, they’re playing different versions of the same game. Some players take risks easily, lose without tightening, and recover without much visible strain. Others don’t have that luxury. They play cautiously, sometimes not because it’s optimal, but because they can’t afford not to. A bad hand for one player is an inconvenience. For another, it changes how every hand plays after it gets played.

At some point, without anyone naming it, the game stops being just about cards and starts becoming about position. Not where you’re sitting physically, but where you’re sitting in relation to risk. Some players can absorb loss. Others have to avoid it. And once that difference exists, the meaning of a “fair game” starts to shift. Because if everyone is following the same rules but not everyone is exposed to the same consequences, it’s not clear what fairness is measuring anymore.

You could argue the game is still fair. The deck is shuffled in the open. The rules don’t change mid-hand. No one is stacking anything. From that perspective, outcomes reflect choices. Some people read the table better. Some people manage risk better. Over time, that’s what separates winning from losing. Blaming the shuffle, then, starts to sound like a refusal to accept that not every loss has something deeper behind it.

But that explanation starts to feel incomplete when the same players seem to stay ahead without ever really being threatened by the downside. The game may be technically the same, but the incentives aren’t. Risk isn’t symmetrical. And once risk behaves differently for different people, the outcomes begin to reflect more than just skill or chance.

This is where the analogy stops being about cards, even if no one says it out loud. Because the question underneath it isn’t really about whether the shuffle was fair. It’s about whether a system can be called fair if it produces outcomes that consistently feel out of reach for some of the people inside it. Not in a dramatic, rigged sense, but in a quieter way, where the distribution of wins and losses starts to look less like randomness and more like a pattern you can’t quite prove.

What makes it complicated is that both interpretations can exist at the same time. The game can be fair in its design and uneven in its experience. The shuffle can be random, and still produce results that don’t feel random depending on where you’re sitting.

Some players really are better. Some players really are just luckier. And some players are working within constraints that never get acknowledged because they aren’t written into the rules.

So when someone asks, “Who shuffled?” it’s not always about the shuffle.

It becomes harder to tell whether you’re just playing the game, or trying to understand the rules that were there long before you sat down.
